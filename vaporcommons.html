Creative Commons: The Super Simple Version

Creative Commons is like a permission slip for your creative stuff.

When you make something (like a photo, song, or writing), you normally get to decide who can use it. Creative Commons is a way to tell people "Hey, you can use my stuff!" while setting some basic rules.

Types of Permission Slips

Attribution (BY)
This means: "You can use my stuff, but you have to say it was mine first!"
Example: Like when you quote a book and mention the author's name.
ShareAlike (SA)
This means: "You can change my stuff, but your new version needs the same rules as mine!"
Example: Like sharing a recipe, but the person who changes it must also let others use their version.
NonCommercial (NC)
This means: "You can use my stuff, but you can't make money from it!"
Example: Like using a photo in your school project, but not in an advertisement.
NoDerivatives (ND)
This means: "You can share my stuff, but you can't change it!"
Example: Like sharing a song exactly as it is, without remixing it.

You can mix and match these rules. For instance, you might say "Attribution + NonCommercial" which means "Tell people I made it and don't make money from it!"

When to Use Creative Commons

  • Want to share your work freely
  • Don't mind others using your stuff
  • Want to be nice to the internet

When NOT to Use Creative Commons

  • Want to keep all rights to yourself
  • Plan to sell your work exclusively
  • Need to protect trade secrets

That's it! Creative Commons is just a simple way to share your stuff while keeping some control over how it's used.
